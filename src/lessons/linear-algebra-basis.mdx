# Linear Algebra — Basis and Change of Basis

<LessonMeta tags={["MATHEMATICS", "LINEAR ALGEBRA", "BASIS", "20–40 MIN"]} />

You've been quietly using a particular basis since the first day you wrote a vector down. When you write $\mathbf{v} = (3, 2)$, you're saying "three of *something*, two of *something else*" — and the something and something-else are the **standard basis vectors** $\hat{\imath} = (1, 0)$ and $\hat{\jmath} = (0, 1)$, the directions that point along the $x$- and $y$-axes. You've internalised this so deeply that the basis itself has become invisible; the coordinates $(3, 2)$ feel like a property *of the vector*, full stop. But the coordinates aren't the vector. The vector is a geometric object — an arrow from the origin — and the coordinates are merely a *recipe* for reconstructing the arrow from a chosen pair of building-block directions. Change the building blocks, and the recipe changes; the arrow doesn't.

This lesson is about that distinction. We'll see that the same geometric vector can have wildly different coordinates depending on which basis you pick, and that the *change of coordinates* between two bases is itself a matrix operation — one whose inverse is exactly the operation that goes back. By the end you should hear "the vector is $(3, 2)$" the way a careful mathematician hears it: *"the vector has coordinates $(3, 2)$ relative to the standard basis"*, with the relative-to clause silent but always present.

## What a basis is

A **basis** for $\mathbb{R}^2$ is a set of two vectors $\{\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}\}$ such that:

1. **They span the plane.** Every vector $\mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^2$ can be written as some linear combination $\mathbf{w} = \alpha \mathbf{u} + \beta \mathbf{v}$.
2. **They are linearly independent.** Neither is a scalar multiple of the other — equivalently, the only way to combine them into the zero vector is $\alpha = \beta = 0$.

Either condition alone isn't enough. Two parallel vectors span only a line, not the plane. Three vectors might span the plane but they're not linearly independent (one is redundant). Exactly two linearly independent vectors form a basis for $\mathbb{R}^2$ — no more, no less.

The two conditions together have a remarkable consequence: **every vector in $\mathbb{R}^2$ has a unique pair of coordinates $(\alpha, \beta)$ relative to a given basis.** Spanning guarantees the coordinates exist; independence guarantees they're unique. (If you could write $\mathbf{w} = \alpha_1 \mathbf{u} + \beta_1 \mathbf{v} = \alpha_2 \mathbf{u} + \beta_2 \mathbf{v}$ in two different ways, subtracting gives $(\alpha_1 - \alpha_2) \mathbf{u} + (\beta_1 - \beta_2) \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$, which by independence forces $\alpha_1 = \alpha_2$ and $\beta_1 = \beta_2$.) Two different bases give you two different unique coordinate systems for the same plane — and that's the whole game.

<Tier level="standard">

> **The standard basis is just a basis.** Nothing makes $\{\hat{\imath}, \hat{\jmath}\}$ canonical except convention. The plane doesn't care which two linearly-independent directions you pick. The choice $\hat{\imath} = (1,0), \hat{\jmath} = (0,1)$ is convenient because it makes the coordinates of every vector trivially readable from its components, but that convenience is paid for by losing the freedom to align the basis to whatever structure the *problem* has. In a problem with a natural diagonal symmetry, the diagonal directions $\mathbf{u} = (1,1)/\sqrt{2}$ and $\mathbf{v} = (-1,1)/\sqrt{2}$ are the "right" basis — the one that makes the problem's structure visible. The standard basis is the *default*, not the *truth*.

</Tier>

## Computing coordinates in a basis — the inverse strikes

How did the widget compute the right-panel coordinates $(\alpha, \beta)$? This is where the previous lesson's machinery pays off. The defining equation

$$
\mathbf{w} = \alpha \mathbf{u} + \beta \mathbf{v}
$$

is a $2 \times 2$ linear system in the unknowns $\alpha, \beta$. Stacking $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ as the columns of a matrix $M = [\mathbf{u} \mid \mathbf{v}]$, the equation becomes

$$
\mathbf{w} = M \begin{pmatrix} \alpha \\ \beta \end{pmatrix},
$$

and solving for $(\alpha, \beta)$ requires inverting $M$:

$$
\begin{pmatrix} \alpha \\ \beta \end{pmatrix} = M^{-1} \mathbf{w}.
$$

This is the deep connection between the inverse lesson and this one. The change-of-basis transformation **is** the matrix $M^{-1}$, where $M$ is the matrix whose columns are the new basis vectors. The basis exists exactly when $M$ is invertible, which happens exactly when $\det M \ne 0$, which happens exactly when $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are linearly independent. Three statements of the same fact, viewed from three different windows — basis-existence, matrix-invertibility, geometric-non-collapse — all wearing the same algebraic fingerprint $\det M \ne 0$.

The collapsed-preset behaviour in the widget is exactly this: when $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are parallel, $\det M = 0$, $M^{-1}$ doesn't exist, and the coordinate tuple is undefined. The widget surfaces the collapse rather than producing a numerically catastrophic answer.

Before pushing on to the matrix-form framing, try the identity with your fingers. The widget below puts you at the helm of a small ship on a grid drawn in the standard basis — but the only label given for the target is its coordinates in a *custom* basis $(\alpha, \beta)$. The controls are also in the custom basis: pressing $+u$ translates the ship by $+\mathbf{u}$ in standard-basis space, $+v$ by $+\mathbf{v}$, and similarly for the negatives. To reach the target you have to execute the equation $\mathbf{T} = \alpha\mathbf{u} + \beta\mathbf{v}$ as a sequence of presses.

Notice what this does to your perception of the target. The target looks like *a specific spot on the grid*, but the only number you have for it is "two in $u$, one in $v$". To get there, you cannot just count grid squares — you have to count along the custom-basis directions, which run diagonally to the standard grid. Each preset puts you in a new basis with the controls in a new language; the ship's pixel-position is the same animal in every basis, but the path to it changes completely.

The "minimum moves" counter is the magic-trick reveal. The smallest number of presses needed is $|\alpha| + |\beta|$ — and the reader who walks the direct route discovers that fact by experience, not by being told. Every move-counter overshoot is the reader's body learning that path-length in the custom basis is what matters, not Euclidean distance on the standard grid. By the time you've solved all four puzzles, you have *enacted* the change-of-basis identity four times over.

A basis is not just a pair of arrows; it's the rule that defines a *whole coordinate grid*. The widget below makes that grid the object of attention. A target lattice is drawn for you — a tilted 2D array of dots and gridlines, generated by some hidden basis you can't see. Your job: drag the $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ handles until your reconstructed lattice (drawn as basis-coloured rings over the target's dots) matches the target. When every target point sits inside one of your rings and every one of your rings has a target point at its centre, you've reconstructed the basis.

There's a small twist that turns out to be the lesson. The *same lattice* can be generated by many different bases. If you find one valid $\{\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}\}$, then $\{\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{v}\}$ generates the same lattice — and so does $\{\mathbf{u}, -\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}\}$, $\{\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{u}\}$, $\{-\mathbf{u}, -\mathbf{v}\}$, and infinitely many others. Concretely: any pair you can reach from the target by multiplying through a $2\times 2$ integer matrix with determinant $\pm 1$ (the **unimodular** matrices — the integer-matrix group whose inverses are also integer matrices) generates the same lattice. The widget detects this and reports your reconstruction matrix $K$ explicitly: "$K$ = identity" when you found the original target, otherwise the integer matrix that takes the target basis to yours. Multiple answers are right; the lattice doesn't care which one you picked.

<GridMorphMatcher />

Two things to notice. First, the "100% match" threshold is achievable from a continuous range of nearby reader-bases — it's a tolerance match, not exact. Second, the unimodular reconstructions are usually easier to find than the original target basis, because the target basis often involves irrational numbers (like $\cos 30°$) that the reader can't dial in by hand, whereas any unimodular cousin of it has cleaner numerical relations. The lesson is structural: in lattices, *the basis is not unique* — only the lattice itself is.

## Change of basis as a transformation

Here's the framing that makes everything click. Let $B$ denote the matrix whose columns are the new basis vectors $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}$. Then:

- $B$ **takes new-basis coordinates and produces standard-basis coordinates**. If you give $B$ the tuple $(\alpha, \beta)$ — i.e. the coordinates "as seen from the custom basis" — multiplying by $B$ gives you $\alpha \mathbf{u} + \beta \mathbf{v}$, which is the standard-basis representation. So $B$ is the *encode-into-standard* operation.

- $B^{-1}$ **takes standard-basis coordinates and produces new-basis coordinates**. If you give $B^{-1}$ the tuple $(x, y)$ — i.e. the coordinates "as seen from the standard basis" — multiplying by $B^{-1}$ gives you the coefficients $(\alpha, \beta)$ such that $\alpha \mathbf{u} + \beta \mathbf{v} = (x, y)$. So $B^{-1}$ is the *decode-into-custom* operation.

These are two halves of the same isomorphism. The plane is *the same plane*; only the coordinate language is changing. $B$ and $B^{-1}$ are the translators between the two languages, each undoing the other.

In matrix form: if $[\mathbf{w}]_{std}$ denotes the standard-basis coordinates of $\mathbf{w}$ and $[\mathbf{w}]_{new}$ denotes its coordinates in the new basis $\{\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}\}$, then

$$
[\mathbf{w}]_{std} = B \cdot [\mathbf{w}]_{new}, \qquad [\mathbf{w}]_{new} = B^{-1} \cdot [\mathbf{w}]_{std}.
$$

Memorise the direction of the arrows. Multiplying by $B$ *goes into* the standard basis; multiplying by $B^{-1}$ *leaves* it. The most common mistake students make at this stage is getting $B$ and $B^{-1}$ confused — and the discipline that prevents it is to always carry the "$[\cdot]_{\text{basis}}$" subscript around for a few weeks until the directionality is internalised.

## Why non-standard bases matter

You might reasonably ask: if everything we just did can be expressed in the standard basis using the formula $M^{-1}\mathbf{w}$, why bother with non-standard bases at all? Why not just compute everything in the standard basis and call it a day?

The answer is structural. **Some bases reveal information that the standard basis hides.** Three big examples that motivate the rest of the linear algebra curriculum:

| Basis | Why it matters | Where it appears |
|---|---|---|
| **Eigenbasis** | A linear transformation $A$ acts diagonally — i.e. just scales each basis vector — when expressed in its eigenbasis. The thing you couldn't easily reason about in the standard basis (a complicated transformation) becomes trivially simple in the right one. | Diagonalisation, spectral theorem, Markov chains, PCA |
| **Orthonormal basis** | A basis of mutually-perpendicular unit vectors. The coordinates in such a basis are exactly the dot products with the basis vectors (no inversion needed), which is computationally cheap and numerically stable. | QR decomposition, Fourier series, signal processing |
| **Principal-component basis** | The basis whose axes point in the directions of maximum variance of a data cloud. Compressing data by truncating coordinates in this basis loses the *least possible information*. | PCA, dimensionality reduction, image compression |

Each of these is "the standard basis for a problem with a particular structure". The basis you pick should be the one that makes the *problem*'s structure visible. When the problem's structure aligns with the standard basis (which it often does in introductory exercises), the standard basis is the right basis. When it doesn't (which it always does in interesting problems), you change basis to align with the structure, do the computation in the easier basis, and — if needed — change back.

The eigenbasis is worth foreshadowing here because it crystallises *why* change-of-basis is the centrepiece of so much of the rest of linear algebra. The idea: for a generic transformation $A$, most directions get *rotated* — $A\mathbf{x}$ points somewhere completely unrelated to $\mathbf{x}$. But some special directions don't rotate at all; $A$ just scales them. Those directions are called **eigenvectors** of $A$, and the basis built out of them is the eigenbasis. In the eigenbasis, $A$ stops looking like a complicated mix of rotation and stretch; it looks like a diagonal matrix — pure scaling per axis.

The widget below makes those special directions findable by inspection. You drag a test vector $\mathbf{x}$; the widget continuously shows $A\mathbf{x}$ as a second arrow. When the two arrows line up (parallel or antiparallel), $A$ is acting on $\mathbf{x}$ as pure scaling, and the widget records the direction with its scale factor $\lambda$. Try the rotation preset first and watch nothing line up — pure rotations have no real eigenvectors, and sweeping $\mathbf{x}$ around the whole plane never produces alignment. Then try the shear: one eigendirection, the shear axis, easy to find. Then the stretch-plus-shear: two eigendirections, one obvious and one subtle.

<EigenbasisHunt />

You're not asked to compute anything. The eigenvalue formula is one lesson down the curriculum. The widget's job is to give you the *visual experience* of the special directions long before you see the formula — so that when "the eigenvectors of $A$" arrives as a definition, it reads as the name for something you've already met.

<Tier level="standard">

> **The change-of-basis matrix is everywhere.** Once you see this pattern, you'll see it constantly. In quantum mechanics, the "position basis" and "momentum basis" are different bases for the same state space, and the Fourier transform is the change-of-basis matrix between them. In computer graphics, the "world coordinates" and the "camera coordinates" are different bases for $\mathbb{R}^3$, and the view matrix is the change-of-basis transformation. In statistics, fitting a model in the "feature basis" versus the "principal-component basis" gives the same model but with different conditioning and interpretability. Anywhere a problem has natural coordinates that differ from the default, change-of-basis is the operation that mediates between them.

</Tier>

## Misconceptions

<Misconception belief="A vector IS its coordinates — when you say w = (3, 2), the tuple (3, 2) is what w is.">

The vector is a *geometric object*: an arrow in the plane, an element of the vector space $\mathbb{R}^2$. The coordinates $(3, 2)$ are a *representation* of that object relative to a particular choice of basis. Change the basis, and the same geometric arrow gets different coordinates; the arrow itself doesn't move. This conflation between "vector" and "coordinates of the vector relative to the standard basis" is harmless in most introductory exercises (because the standard basis is always implicit and never changes), but it becomes actively wrong the moment you start working with multiple bases — which you will, the moment eigenvectors or PCA show up. The right mental model: a vector is a basis-independent object; coordinates are a basis-relative description of it.

</Misconception>

<Misconception belief="Any two non-zero vectors form a basis for the plane.">

Not quite. The two vectors must be **linearly independent** — neither can be a scalar multiple of the other. Two parallel non-zero vectors point along the same line and span only that line, not the plane. The widget's "Collapsed (parallel)" preset shows this directly: $\mathbf{u} = (1, 1)$ and $\mathbf{v} = (-1, -1)$ are both non-zero, but they don't form a basis. The diagnostic is the determinant: $\det[\mathbf{u} \mid \mathbf{v}] = 0$ exactly when the vectors fail to be linearly independent. Two vectors form a basis if and only if both are non-zero *and* their determinant is non-zero.

</Misconception>

The fastest way to internalise "linearly independent" as a visual reflex — separate from any algebra — is to be made to call it on pairs of vectors under mild pressure. The tournament below shows you a pair, gives you two buttons (is a basis / is not a basis), and advances if you get it right. Get it wrong and the run ends; you see the determinant, the rationale, and you start over. Ten rounds clear the tournament; the difficulty grows quietly with the round number — early pairs are obviously independent or obviously parallel, late pairs are near-collinear configurations that look fine until you check the slope ratio.

Treat it as a calibration drill rather than a quiz. The point is not to grind out a win; the point is to feel what your eyes do when a pair is collinear-but-close to looking independent. By round seven or eight you'll catch yourself slowing down to check ratios — exactly the move the misconception above warns about. The widget makes the warning physical.

## The general definition — bases in $\mathbb{R}^n$ and beyond

Everything generalises. A basis for $\mathbb{R}^n$ is any set of $n$ linearly independent vectors that span $\mathbb{R}^n$. Two key invariants:

- **Every basis for $\mathbb{R}^n$ has exactly $n$ vectors.** Not $n-1$, not $n+1$. The number $n$ is called the **dimension** of the space, and it's a basis-independent number — every basis has exactly this many vectors. This is one of the foundational theorems of linear algebra.

- **Any $n$ linearly independent vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$ automatically span $\mathbb{R}^n$.** And conversely, any $n$ vectors that span $\mathbb{R}^n$ are automatically linearly independent. Above the threshold $n$, vectors become redundant; below $n$, they fail to span. The number $n$ is the exact threshold.

The change-of-basis machinery scales identically. If $B$ is an $n \times n$ matrix whose columns are an alternative basis for $\mathbb{R}^n$, then $B$ takes new-basis coordinates to standard-basis coordinates and $B^{-1}$ goes the other way. The 2-dimensional case you just worked through is the smallest non-trivial example; the algebra extends without modification to 3 dimensions, 256 dimensions, or 16,384 dimensions (the dimension of a CLIP embedding — yes, change-of-basis applies there too).

The notion also extends beyond $\mathbb{R}^n$. Vector spaces of polynomials, of continuous functions, of matrices themselves — all have bases (sometimes infinite-dimensional ones), and change-of-basis machinery applies in essentially the same form. The Fourier basis for periodic functions, the Hermite-polynomial basis for the Hilbert space of quantum mechanics, the wavelet basis for signal processing — all are non-standard bases for function spaces, and change-of-basis is the operation that translates between them.

</Tier>

## Where to read next

This lesson sets up the substantial conceptual leap into the structure of vector spaces: bases, dimension, rank, null spaces, and ultimately eigenvectors and decompositions.

- **Linear Algebra — Rank, Null Space, and the Four Fundamental Subspaces** (forthcoming) — the rank of a matrix is the number of linearly independent columns, which is the dimension of the basis they form. The null space is the set of vectors that get mapped to zero — also a subspace with its own basis. The four fundamental subspaces (row, column, null, left-null) are the structural anatomy of any matrix, and bases are the language for describing them.
- **Linear Algebra — Eigenvectors and Diagonalisation** (forthcoming) — the eigenbasis of a matrix $A$ is the basis in which $A$ is diagonal. The change-of-basis matrix between the standard basis and the eigenbasis is the matrix $P$ in $A = PDP^{-1}$, and the diagonal $D$ is what $A$ "really" does to vectors — scaling along the eigendirections. This lesson is the prerequisite that makes eigenvalues make sense.
- **[Linear Algebra — Matrix Inverse and Gaussian Elimination](#/lesson/linear-algebra-matrix-inverse)** — re-read with the new framing. The change-of-basis matrix $B^{-1}$ that you just used is exactly the same kind of object as the inverses computed in that lesson. Now you have a concrete reason to care.
