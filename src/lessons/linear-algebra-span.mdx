# Linear Algebra — Linear Combinations and Span

<LessonMeta tags={["MATHEMATICS", "LINEAR ALGEBRA", "SPAN", "12–28 MIN"]} />

Up to this point, every vector you've worked with has been a *specific* point — one arrow with one tip at one location. Useful, but limiting. The interesting questions in linear algebra rarely have the form "where is this one vector?" They have the form "given a *set* of vectors, what can I build with them?" That's the question this lesson is built around.

Two ideas live here, and they're inseparable. The first is the **linear combination** — the act of taking a few vectors and mixing them together with scalar coefficients. The second is the **span** — the *set* of every point you can reach by varying those coefficients freely. The first is an operation; the second is the result of running that operation across all possible inputs and collecting the results. Together they turn linear algebra from "operations on individual vectors" into the **geometry of vector spaces**. From this lesson onward, almost every concept — basis, rank, dimension, column space, eigenvectors — is built on top of span. It's the foundational object, and the widget on this page is designed to make it visible.

**Prerequisites:** [Linear Algebra — Foundations](#/lesson/linear-algebra-foundations) (vector addition, scalar multiplication). The two operations on this page are nothing more than those two operations applied together, repeatedly, over varying scalar choices. If the foundations don't feel concrete yet, start there.

## The linear combination

Given two vectors $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$, a **linear combination** of them is anything of the form:

$$
\alpha \mathbf{u} + \beta \mathbf{v}
$$

where $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are scalars. That's the whole definition. Scale each vector independently, then add the results. You've actually been doing this for a while — every time scalar multiplication and vector addition combined in the foundations lesson, that was a linear combination, just not by name.

The interesting question is not "how do I compute one" — that's mechanical. The interesting question is the one you can only ask once you've named the *operation*: **what *set* of points can you reach by varying $\alpha$ and $\beta$ over all real numbers?** That set has a name. It's called the **span** of $\{\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}\}$, and the widget below makes it directly visible.

<LinearCombination />

The faint green dots in the widget are the span, sampled at a grid of $(\alpha, \beta)$ values from $-2$ to $2$ along each axis. The pattern they trace out is what you want to watch. Two situations to feel by dragging $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$:

- **When $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ point in genuinely different directions**, the dots fill the whole plane. Every point in $\mathbb{R}^2$ is reachable as some $\alpha\mathbf{u} + \beta\mathbf{v}$. The span is *all of* $\mathbb{R}^2$.
- **When $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ point in the same (or opposite) direction**, the dots collapse onto a single line through the origin. Most of the plane is suddenly unreachable. The span is a one-dimensional line.

The widget colour-codes this distinction: the readout panel turns green when the vectors are independent, red when dependent. The output vector $\alpha\mathbf{u} + \beta\mathbf{v}$ also turns red in the dependent case — a signal that your free-coefficient exploration has been trapped on a line through the origin, and no matter what $\alpha$ and $\beta$ you pick, you can't escape it.

## Linear independence

The distinction the widget keeps surfacing has a name. Two vectors $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are **linearly independent** when neither is a scalar multiple of the other — they point in genuinely different directions and together they reach more of the plane than either could alone. They are **linearly dependent** when one *is* a scalar multiple of the other — they live on the same line through the origin, and the second one adds no new reachable direction to the span.

The algebraic test in two dimensions is short, and it's a number you already know. Take the determinant of the matrix whose columns are $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$:

$$
\det \begin{pmatrix} u_1 & v_1 \\ u_2 & v_2 \end{pmatrix} = u_1 v_2 - u_2 v_1
$$

A non-zero determinant means the vectors are independent and the span fills the plane. A zero determinant means they're dependent and the span is a line.

<Tier level="standard">

Here's where the lesson on matrices and this lesson connect. **The determinant from the matrices lesson is the same number as the independence test here.** When you compute $\det A$, you can read the number two ways: as the area scaling factor of the transformation $A$, or as the linear-dependence test for $A$'s columns. The two interpretations are not coincidences — they're the same fact. If the columns of $A$ are linearly dependent (one is a scalar multiple of the other), then $A$ collapses the unit square onto the line they share, so the parallelogram has zero area, so the determinant is zero. Three ways of saying one thing.

</Tier>

<Misconception belief="Linear dependence is just about the two vectors being identical.">

Dependence is broader. $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are dependent whenever **one is any scalar multiple of the other** — including negative multiples (so $\mathbf{u}$ and $-\mathbf{u}$ are dependent), fractional multiples ($\mathbf{u}$ and $0.5\mathbf{u}$), and the zero-vector edge case ($\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{0}$ are technically dependent because $\mathbf{0} = 0 \cdot \mathbf{u}$). The right phrasing isn't "identical" — it's "they live on the same line through the origin."

</Misconception>

## Span beyond two vectors

The widget on this page is limited to two vectors because we're in $\mathbb{R}^2$ and that's the maximum useful number for spanning the plane. The pattern generalises in the obvious way, and the geometric pictures keep working in higher dimensions:

- In $\mathbb{R}^3$: *one* vector spans a line through the origin; *two* (independent) vectors span a plane through the origin; *three* (independent) vectors span all of $\mathbb{R}^3$.
- In $\mathbb{R}^n$: $k$ linearly independent vectors span a $k$-dimensional subspace. You can never span more than $n$ dimensions with vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$ — and the maximum $n$ is what we mean by "the dimension of $\mathbb{R}^n$".

The formal definition of linear independence for more than two vectors is worth writing out, because it's the form you'll see in textbooks from here on. A set of $k$ vectors $\{\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k\}$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$ is **linearly independent** if the only way to write the zero vector as a linear combination

$$
c_1 \mathbf{v}_1 + c_2 \mathbf{v}_2 + \ldots + c_k \mathbf{v}_k = \mathbf{0}
$$

is with all $c_i = 0$. Dependence means *some* non-trivial choice of $c_i$'s makes the combination zero — which is just a fancy way of saying *some* combination of all-but-one of the vectors expresses the remaining one. In plain language: at least one of the vectors is redundant, contributing no new direction the others can't already build.

## Span and the columns of a matrix

The columns of a $2 \times 2$ matrix $A$ are two vectors in $\mathbb{R}^2$. The set of every vector you can write as $A\mathbf{x}$ for some $\mathbf{x}$ is called the **column space** of $A$ — and the column space is exactly the *span* of $A$'s columns. The connection between this lesson and the matrices lesson is now mechanical:

- If $A$'s columns are linearly independent ($\det A \ne 0$), the column space is all of $\mathbb{R}^2$. Every vector in the plane is reachable as $A\mathbf{x}$ for some $\mathbf{x}$. The matrix $A$ is **invertible** — you can go backwards from any output to its unique input.
- If $A$'s columns are linearly dependent ($\det A = 0$), the column space is a line. Only vectors on that line are reachable as $A\mathbf{x}$ at all. The matrix $A$ is **singular** — given a target vector not on that line, there's no input that produces it; given a target on the line, there are infinitely many inputs that produce it. Either way, you can't invert.

Three different vocabularies for the same single fact: linear dependence of columns, zero determinant, non-invertibility. They're not three separate properties that happen to co-occur — they're three vocabularies for one structural property of the matrix.
