# Linear Algebra — Matrices as Transformations

<LessonMeta tags={["MATHEMATICS", "LINEAR ALGEBRA", "MATRICES", "15–35 MIN"]} />

A **matrix** is a rectangular grid of scalars — but that algebraic description undersells what a matrix actually does. A $2 \times 2$ matrix is a **linear transformation of the plane**: it eats every point in $\mathbb{R}^2$ and spits out a new point, in a way that preserves three things — the origin stays at the origin, lines stay lines (no curves), and parallel lines stay parallel. This lesson builds the geometric intuition for matrices through one widget you'll touch over and over.

<Tier level="standard">

**Prerequisites:** this lesson assumes you've already worked through *Linear Algebra — Scalars, Vectors, Matrices* (the foundations lesson). If "vector" and "scalar multiplication" don't feel concrete yet, start there first.

</Tier>

## The 2×2 matrix

A $2 \times 2$ matrix has 4 entries:

$$
A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}
$$

Algebraically, a matrix and a vector multiply like this:

$$
A \mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} ax + by \\ cx + dy \end{pmatrix}
$$

That's the calculation. The geometric meaning is more revealing: a $2 \times 2$ matrix is fully specified by **where it sends the basis vectors**. The standard basis is $\hat{\imath} = (1, 0)$ and $\hat{\jmath} = (0, 1)$. After the transformation:

- $\hat{\imath} \mapsto (a, c)$ — the matrix's first column
- $\hat{\jmath} \mapsto (b, d)$ — the matrix's second column

Every other point in the plane is some combination of $\hat{\imath}$ and $\hat{\jmath}$, so once you know where the basis vectors go, the rest of the plane follows by linearity. **A $2 \times 2$ matrix is fully specified by where it sends $\hat{\imath}$ and $\hat{\jmath}$.**

## Determinants — the area-scaling factor

The **determinant** of a $2 \times 2$ matrix tells you how the matrix scales **areas**:

$$
\det A = ad - bc
$$

Geometrically: the unit square has area 1; after the transformation it becomes a parallelogram whose (signed) area is exactly $\det A$. Three special cases worth feeling:

- $\det A = 1$: the transformation preserves area exactly. Rotations and reflections are like this.
- $\det A = 0$: the transformation **collapses** the plane onto a line (or a point). The unit square's image has zero area. The matrix is called **singular** in this case — it can't be inverted, because once you've collapsed the plane, no transformation can recover the original.
- $\det A < 0$: the transformation **flips orientation** — the unit square's image traversed in the original order goes around the parallelogram in the opposite direction. Reflections give this; pure rotations don't.

<Misconception belief="A matrix with determinant zero just has a really small effect.">

A determinant of zero means the matrix is **singular** — the transformation collapses the entire plane onto a lower-dimensional subspace (a line, or in the $\mathbf{0}$ matrix case, a single point). All of $\mathbb{R}^2$ now lives in a 1D image. This is a fundamentally different regime from "small effect" — it's an *irreversible* operation. You can't recover the original vector from its image because infinitely many original vectors map to the same image point. Algebraically: singular matrices have no inverse.

</Misconception>

## Composition of transformations is matrix multiplication

Applying transformation $A$ then transformation $B$ to a vector $\mathbf{v}$ gives $B(A\mathbf{v})$. The associativity of matrix multiplication means this equals $(BA)\mathbf{v}$ — there's a single matrix $BA$ that captures the combined transformation. **Matrix multiplication is exactly the algebraic encoding of composing geometric transformations.**

Order matters: $AB \ne BA$ in general. Rotating first and then shearing is different from shearing first and then rotating — try it on a unit square in your head. The matrix-composition lesson (forthcoming) makes this concrete with a side-by-side widget that shows $AB$ and $BA$ simultaneously.
