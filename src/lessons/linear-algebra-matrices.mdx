# Linear Algebra — Matrices as Transformations

<LessonMeta tags={["MATHEMATICS", "LINEAR ALGEBRA", "MATRICES", "15–35 MIN"]} />

Imagine you're holding a flat sheet of paper printed with a grid of dots. You can pinch one edge and pull it sideways — the dots shear into a diamond pattern. You can grab opposite corners and stretch the sheet — the dots spread apart. You can spin the sheet around a pin through its centre — the dots rotate. Every one of those moves is a **transformation of the plane**: it takes the original arrangement of points and produces a new one, in a way that preserves certain things (the dots stay flat, they stay on a uniform grid, no curves appear) and changes others (their positions, their orientations, their separations). Matrices are the mathematical language for describing exactly those transformations.

A **matrix** is, on its surface, just a rectangular grid of scalars. But that description radically undersells what a matrix *does*. A $2 \times 2$ matrix is a linear transformation of the plane — it consumes every point in $\mathbb{R}^2$ and produces a new point, preserving three properties along the way: the origin stays at the origin, lines stay lines (no curves ever appear), and parallel lines stay parallel. This lesson builds the geometric intuition for matrices through one widget you'll touch over and over until "matrix" stops meaning "grid of numbers" in your head and starts meaning "way of warping space".

## The 2×2 matrix

A $2 \times 2$ matrix has four entries, arranged in a square:

$$
A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}
$$

Algebraically, a matrix and a vector multiply by the following rule — the calculation is mechanical and you've probably seen it before:

$$
A \mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} ax + by \\ cx + dy \end{pmatrix}
$$

That's the calculation. The geometric meaning is much more revealing, and once you internalise it, you'll never need to do the arithmetic step-by-step again to predict what a matrix is going to do.

Here's the move worth feeling: **a $2 \times 2$ matrix is completely specified by where it sends the basis vectors**. The standard basis in $\mathbb{R}^2$ is $\hat{\imath} = (1, 0)$ (the unit vector along the x-axis) and $\hat{\jmath} = (0, 1)$ (the unit vector along the y-axis). After the transformation:

- $\hat{\imath} \mapsto (a, c)$ — the matrix's first column.
- $\hat{\jmath} \mapsto (b, d)$ — the matrix's second column.

Every other point in the plane is some specific combination of $\hat{\imath}$ and $\hat{\jmath}$ — that's literally what coordinates *are*. So once you know where the basis vectors go, the rest of the plane follows automatically by linearity. **A $2 \times 2$ matrix is fully specified by where it sends $\hat{\imath}$ and $\hat{\jmath}$, and those two destinations are sitting right there as the matrix's columns.** Read the columns; you've read the transformation.

The cyan arrow is the transformed $\hat{\imath}$; the yellow arrow is the transformed $\hat{\jmath}$. Move the sliders, watch the columns drag the basis vectors around, watch the whole parallelogram follow.

## Determinants — the area-scaling factor

There's a single number you can compute from a $2 \times 2$ matrix that tells you almost everything about *what kind of transformation* the matrix is. That number is the **determinant**:

$$
\det A = ad - bc
$$

The geometric meaning is the cleanest thing in linear algebra. The unit square has area 1. After the transformation, it becomes a parallelogram. The parallelogram's signed area is *exactly* $\det A$. That's not a coincidence or an approximation — the formula $ad - bc$ is *what* the area of that parallelogram is, expressed in terms of the matrix entries.

Three special cases worth feeling in your hands.

- $\det A = 1$ — the transformation preserves area exactly. The parallelogram has the same area as the unit square, even though its shape can be radically different. Rotations and shears are in this category.
- $\det A = 0$ — the transformation **collapses** the plane onto a lower-dimensional subspace. The unit square's image has zero area, which means it has to be a line segment (or in the degenerate case where the whole matrix is zero, a single point). A matrix with zero determinant is called **singular**, and it has the property that there's no way to undo it — once you've collapsed the plane onto a line, no transformation can recover the original arrangement of points, because infinitely many original points map to each image point.
- $\det A < 0$ — the transformation **flips orientation**. The unit square's vertices, traversed in their original order, go around the image parallelogram in the *opposite* direction (clockwise vs anticlockwise). Reflections give this; pure rotations don't. The fill colour in the widget below flips when the determinant goes negative — that visual cue is the orientation-reversal made literal.

<Misconception belief="A matrix with determinant zero just has a really small effect.">

A determinant of zero means the matrix is **singular** — the transformation collapses the entire plane onto a lower-dimensional subspace (a line, or in the $\mathbf{0}$ matrix case, a single point). All of $\mathbb{R}^2$ now lives in a 1D image. This is a fundamentally different regime from "small effect" — it's an *irreversible* operation. You can't recover the original vector from its image because infinitely many original vectors map to the same image point. Algebraically: singular matrices have no inverse.

</Misconception>

### Singularity is a knife edge

Here's something worth feeling in your hands. The space of $2 \times 2$ matrices is four-dimensional — each matrix is a choice of four numbers $(a, b, c, d)$. Of all those choices, how many produce a singular matrix? The condition is $ad - bc = 0$ — a single equation in four unknowns, which carves out a *three-dimensional* surface inside the four-dimensional ambient space. Singular matrices form a set of measure zero. Pick a random matrix from a continuous distribution and you'll get a non-singular one with probability one.

Said more vividly: singularity is a knife edge. The two columns of $A$ — those basis-vector images $A\hat{\imath}$ and $A\hat{\jmath}$ — have to be *exactly* linearly dependent, one a precise scalar multiple of the other. A hair off in any direction and you're back in the invertible regime. The widget below is the experiential version of that fact. Drag the two basis vectors until $\det A$ drops to zero (within $0.005$). You won't get it on the first try. You'll get close, the "near miss" indicator will flash, then a small adjustment will push you back out. That difficulty *is* the lesson — the singular set is genuinely thin.

## The named-transformation dictionary

Reading a matrix off a picture is the harder direction. The easier direction — going from a *named* transformation like "rotate by 30°" or "shear-x by 1" to the four matrix entries — is the one you'll use far more often in practice. It's the move you make every time you write down a matrix from a verbal description, every time you sanity-check a calculation against the geometric label, every time you read a colleague's diff and want to know what their matrix is doing.

It's also the move that has nothing clever about it. Every named transformation has a small closed-form recipe — rotation by $\theta$ is $\begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{pmatrix}$, shear-x by $k$ is $\begin{pmatrix} 1 & k \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$, scale-y by $s$ is $\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & s \end{pmatrix}$, reflection across the *x*-axis is $\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}$. The dictionary is small enough to memorise, and the matching game below cements the lookup by forcing six commitments at once: drag a line from each transformation name on the left to the matrix on the right that produces it.

The trap to name: half the wrong matches will look almost right because they share three of four entries with the correct matrix. The difference between "rotate 30°" and "scale 2× vertically" lives in the off-diagonal entries — get the columns wrong and you've named the wrong transformation. The columns of $A$ are still the images of $\hat{\imath}$ and $\hat{\jmath}$; the matching game is just the dictionary version of that same identity.

## Matrix-vector multiplication — a transformation in motion

So far we've watched matrices act on the unit square — but the unit square is just a visualisation aid. What a matrix actually does, mechanically, is consume one specific vector $\mathbf{v}$ and produce one specific vector $A\mathbf{v}$. Every vector in the plane gets its own destination. The unit square just packages four particularly informative destinations (the four corners) into a single picture.

## Composition of transformations is matrix multiplication

Apply transformation $A$ to a vector $\mathbf{v}$, then apply transformation $B$ to the result. You get $B(A\mathbf{v})$. Matrix multiplication is exactly the operation that captures this in one step: the associativity of matrix multiplication means $B(A\mathbf{v}) = (BA)\mathbf{v}$, so there's a single matrix $BA$ that does both transformations at once. **Matrix multiplication isn't a procedure invented for its own sake; it's the algebraic encoding of composing geometric transformations**, and the order in which you multiply mirrors the order in which the transformations apply.

The order matters. $AB \ne BA$ in general. Rotate first and then shear: you get one final shape. Shear first and then rotate: you get a different final shape. Try it in your head with a unit square. Rotate it 30° anticlockwise and *then* shear it horizontally — the shear acts on a tilted square. Shear it horizontally and *then* rotate 30° — the rotation acts on a parallelogram that was already non-square. The two operations don't commute, and that non-commutativity is one of the most consequential facts in all of linear algebra.
