# Linear Algebra — The Dot Product

<LessonMeta tags={["MATHEMATICS", "LINEAR ALGEBRA", "DOT PRODUCT", "12–30 MIN"]} />

Two vectors walk into a multiplication and find out the operation doesn't quite work. You can't take a 2-tuple, multiply it by another 2-tuple, and get back a 2-tuple in any way that has consistent geometric meaning — every candidate definition runs into problems. Linear algebra has *several* operations that take two vectors as input, but each one outputs something different: a scalar, a third vector, a matrix. The most fundamental of those — the one you'll meet first and use most — is the **dot product**, and what it gives you is a single number that measures *how much two vectors agree*.

That phrasing — "how much they agree" — is what this lesson is built to make precise. You'll see the dot product two ways: as an algebraic formula that's mechanical to compute, and as a geometric projection that's where all the intuition lives. The bridge between them — the fact that the same number drops out of both pictures — is the dot product's central result.

## The two faces of the dot product

The dot product has two definitions, and they're equivalent. You'll use both, often in the same conversation.

**Algebraic definition** — multiply components, then add:

$$
\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = a_1 b_1 + a_2 b_2
$$

**Geometric definition** — length times length times the cosine of the angle between the two vectors:

$$
\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = |\mathbf{a}| \, |\mathbf{b}| \cos\theta
$$

Take a moment to register how different these two formulas *look*. One uses component arithmetic; the other uses magnitudes and angles. They don't even seem to be about the same thing. And yet — and this is the main result — **they produce the same number for any two vectors**. The proof goes via the law of cosines applied to the triangle formed by $\mathbf{a}$, $\mathbf{b}$, and $\mathbf{a} - \mathbf{b}$, and it's worth doing once on paper if you haven't; the equivalence isn't a coincidence.

You'll use the algebraic form to compute, and you'll use the geometric form to think. That division of labour is the dot product's whole pedagogical pattern.

## The shadow on the line

The geometric form has a visual reading that's worth burning into your visual memory. Draw the two vectors $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$ starting at the same origin. Drop a perpendicular from the tip of $\mathbf{a}$ onto the line through $\mathbf{b}$. The foot of that perpendicular is at signed distance $|\mathbf{a}| \cos\theta$ from the origin, measured along $\mathbf{b}$'s direction. That signed distance is the **shadow** of $\mathbf{a}$ on $\mathbf{b}$.

And the dot product is exactly that shadow length times $|\mathbf{b}|$:

$$
\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = (|\mathbf{a}| \cos\theta) \cdot |\mathbf{b}|
$$

So when you compute a dot product, you're literally measuring "how far does the shadow of $\mathbf{a}$ stretch along $\mathbf{b}$'s direction, scaled by $\mathbf{b}$'s length". That sentence is the dot product in one phrase. Hold onto it; everything that follows is variations on it.

A few patterns to feel as you drag:

- **Vectors aligned**: the shadow of $\mathbf{a}$ reaches all the way to $\mathbf{a}$'s tip (the foot of the perpendicular *is* $\mathbf{a}$). $\cos\theta = 1$, dot product = $|\mathbf{a}| \cdot |\mathbf{b}|$. Maximum possible value for the given magnitudes.
- **Vectors perpendicular**: the shadow collapses to the origin. The foot of the perpendicular *is* the origin. $\cos\theta = 0$, dot product = 0. No agreement at all.
- **Vectors anti-aligned**: the shadow stretches in the *opposite* direction from $\mathbf{b}$. $\cos\theta = -1$, dot product is the *negative* of the product of magnitudes. Maximum disagreement.

This last point — that the dot product can be **zero** for two non-zero vectors — is the dot product's most useful single property. It gives you a clean algebraic test for perpendicularity: $\mathbf{a} \perp \mathbf{b}$ exactly when $\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = 0$. Two perpendicular vectors are vectors whose shadows on each other are zero — they have nothing to project onto each other, no agreement, no overlap.

</Tier>

## Properties worth knowing

The dot product has three structural properties that make it usable as a building block in larger constructions. Each one has an algebraic version that's easy to verify and a geometric reading that explains *why* it has to hold.

- **Commutative**: $\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = \mathbf{b} \cdot \mathbf{a}$. Algebraically obvious (multiplication of scalars is commutative). Geometrically: the shadow of $\mathbf{a}$ on $\mathbf{b}$ has signed length $|\mathbf{a}| \cos\theta$; the shadow of $\mathbf{b}$ on $\mathbf{a}$ has signed length $|\mathbf{b}| \cos\theta$. The angle $\theta$ between them is the same either way. Multiply each shadow by the other vector's length and you get the same scalar.
- **Distributive over vector addition**: $\mathbf{a} \cdot (\mathbf{b} + \mathbf{c}) = \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} + \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{c}$. Falls out of the component definition immediately. Geometrically: projecting onto $\mathbf{a}$'s direction is a linear operation, and linearity respects vector addition.
- **Compatible with scalar multiplication**: $(k\mathbf{a}) \cdot \mathbf{b} = k (\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b})$. Stretch $\mathbf{a}$ by $k$, and its shadow on $\mathbf{b}$ stretches by exactly the same $k$.

Together these three make the dot product a **bilinear form** — linear in each argument when the other is fixed. That property is exactly what lets the dot product compose cleanly with matrix-vector multiplication, where each entry of $A\mathbf{v}$ is a dot product between a row of $A$ and the vector $\mathbf{v}$.

<Misconception belief="If a · b = 0, then either a is zero, b is zero, or both.">

The dot product can be zero even when **both vectors are non-zero** — that's precisely the orthogonality case. $(1, 0) \cdot (0, 1) = 0$ even though both vectors have magnitude 1. The dot product measures *agreement*, not magnitude; two non-zero vectors can simply have no agreement (i.e., be perpendicular).

</Misconception>

## Magnitudes and angles from dot products

Two consequences of the dot product worth memorising — they fall out of the geometric definition almost for free, but they're useful constantly.

**Vector magnitude.** $|\mathbf{a}|^2 = \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{a}$. A vector dotted with itself gives its squared magnitude. Geometrically: the shadow of $\mathbf{a}$ on itself is exactly $\mathbf{a}$ (the perpendicular drop lands at $\mathbf{a}$'s tip), and the geometric formula collapses to $|\mathbf{a}| \cdot |\mathbf{a}| \cos 0 = |\mathbf{a}|^2$. This is *how* you compute magnitude from components — $|\mathbf{a}| = \sqrt{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{a}}$ — and it generalises to any dimension.

**Angle between two vectors.** Rearrange the geometric form:

$$
\cos\theta = \frac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{|\mathbf{a}| \, |\mathbf{b}|}
$$

This is *the* way to compute the angle between two arbitrary vectors. In two or three dimensions you could draw the vectors and measure with a protractor, but that's not how it's done in practice; the formula above gives you $\cos\theta$ directly, and the angle follows from $\arccos$.

The same formula works in $\mathbb{R}^n$ for any $n$ — take the dot product (sum of $n$ component products), divide by the product of magnitudes (each computed as $\sqrt{\mathbf{x} \cdot \mathbf{x}}$). High-dimensional vectors don't *visually* have an angle, but they have a *cosine of an angle*, and that cosine is what most "similarity" measures in modern machine learning actually compute. Two CLIP embeddings have an angle in 512-dimensional space; the cosine of that angle is what nearly every embedding-similarity comparison is built on. It's also what A-FINE's fidelity head extends — not just the cosine, but a richer SSIM-style structural similarity that the dot product is the simplest version of.

## Projection — the operation, not just the geometry

So far the "shadow" has been a visualisation aid. But the shadow is also a *vector operation* with its own name: the **projection** of $\mathbf{a}$ onto $\mathbf{b}$. It's the vector pointing along $\mathbf{b}$'s direction whose magnitude equals the signed shadow length:

$$
\text{proj}_{\mathbf{b}} \mathbf{a} = \frac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{|\mathbf{b}|^2} \, \mathbf{b}
$$

Read the formula one factor at a time. The scalar coefficient $\dfrac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{|\mathbf{b}|^2}$ is the *projection scalar* — what the widget above displays as the "projection scalar" when you drag the vectors. Multiplying that scalar by $\mathbf{b}$ converts it into a vector lying on $\mathbf{b}$'s line, with the right length and direction to be the shadow.

Projection is one of those operations that you'll meet again in nearly every direction you go. It underlies **least-squares regression** (where you project the observed data onto the column space of the design matrix). It underlies **Gram-Schmidt orthogonalisation** (which subtracts projections to build an orthonormal basis out of an arbitrary one). It underlies the **singular value decomposition** (which decomposes any matrix into a sequence of rotations and stretches along orthogonal projection directions). Once you've got the projection formula here, all of those downstream operations have one less moving part to learn.

<Misconception belief={"proj_b(a) = proj_a(b) — projection is symmetric, like the dot product."}>

The **dot product** is commutative — $\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = \mathbf{b} \cdot \mathbf{a}$. The **projection operation** is not. Projecting $\mathbf{a}$ onto $\mathbf{b}$ gives a vector along $\mathbf{b}$'s direction; projecting $\mathbf{b}$ onto $\mathbf{a}$ gives a vector along $\mathbf{a}$'s direction. The two results live on different lines (unless $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$ are already parallel).

Concrete example: with $\mathbf{a} = (1, 2)$ and $\mathbf{b} = (3, 0)$:

$$
\text{proj}_{\mathbf{b}}\mathbf{a} = \frac{3}{9}(3, 0) = (1, 0), \qquad \text{proj}_{\mathbf{a}}\mathbf{b} = \frac{3}{5}(1, 2) = (0.6, 1.2).
$$

Two different vectors, on two different lines, of two different magnitudes. The dot product *factor* $\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = 3$ appears in both numerators (because it's symmetric), but the denominator and the post-multiplied vector are different. The asymmetry isn't a quirk — it's a consequence of "shadow on a line" depending on *which line*.

</Misconception>

## A worked angle calculation

Two unit vectors $\mathbf{u} = (1, 0)$ and $\mathbf{v} = (\cos 60°, \sin 60°) = (0.5, 0.866)$. The dot product is $1 \cdot 0.5 + 0 \cdot 0.866 = 0.5$. Both magnitudes are 1, so $\cos\theta = 0.5 / (1 \cdot 1) = 0.5$, which means $\theta = 60°$. The formula reproduces the angle we built into $\mathbf{v}$'s construction — exactly. Doing this calculation by hand once is the moment the algebraic-and-geometric equivalence stops being a slogan and becomes muscle memory.
