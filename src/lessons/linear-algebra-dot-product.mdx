# Linear Algebra — The Dot Product

<LessonMeta tags={["MATHEMATICS", "LINEAR ALGEBRA", "DOT PRODUCT", "12–30 MIN"]} />

There's no straightforward way to "multiply" two vectors in $\mathbb{R}^2$. You can't multiply a 2-tuple by another 2-tuple and get a 2-tuple in any way that's geometrically meaningful — the operation either has to *output something different* (a scalar) or *use a different rule entirely*. The **dot product** is the first of those choices, and it's the most fundamental: it takes two vectors and produces a single scalar that captures *how much they agree*.

</Tier>

## The two faces of the dot product

The dot product has two equivalent definitions — one algebraic, one geometric — and the equivalence between them is the *content* of the operation.

**Algebraic definition:** multiply components and add.

$$
\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = a_1 b_1 + a_2 b_2
$$

**Geometric definition:** length times length times the cosine of the angle between them.

$$
\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = |\mathbf{a}| \, |\mathbf{b}| \cos\theta
$$

That these *two definitions describe the same number* is the dot product's main result. The algebraic version is what you compute with; the geometric version is what you *think* with.

## The shadow on the line

The geometric form has a visual reading worth memorising. Draw the two vectors $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$ at the same origin. Drop a perpendicular from the tip of $\mathbf{a}$ onto the line through $\mathbf{b}$. The foot of that perpendicular is at signed distance $|\mathbf{a}| \cos\theta$ from the origin along $\mathbf{b}$'s direction — this is the **shadow** of $\mathbf{a}$ on $\mathbf{b}$. The dot product is exactly that signed shadow length times $|\mathbf{b}|$:

$$
\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = (|\mathbf{a}| \cos\theta) \cdot |\mathbf{b}|
$$

<Tier level="standard">

A few patterns to feel by dragging:

- **Vectors aligned**: shadow equals $\mathbf{a}$ (the foot of the perpendicular is at $\mathbf{a}$'s tip). $\cos\theta = 1$, dot product = $|\mathbf{a}| \cdot |\mathbf{b}|$.
- **Vectors perpendicular**: shadow collapses to the origin. The foot of the perpendicular IS the origin. $\cos\theta = 0$, dot product = 0.
- **Vectors anti-aligned**: shadow points in the *opposite* direction from $\mathbf{b}$. $\cos\theta = -1$, dot product is the *negative* of $|\mathbf{a}| \cdot |\mathbf{b}|$.

The dot product is **the** test for orthogonality: $\mathbf{a} \perp \mathbf{b}$ exactly when $\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = 0$. Two perpendicular vectors have nothing to project onto each other.

</Tier>

## Properties worth knowing

The dot product has three structural properties that make it useful as a building block:

- **Commutative**: $\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = \mathbf{b} \cdot \mathbf{a}$. The shadow of $\mathbf{a}$ on $\mathbf{b}$ has signed length $|\mathbf{a}| \cos\theta$; the shadow of $\mathbf{b}$ on $\mathbf{a}$ has signed length $|\mathbf{b}| \cos\theta$. Multiplied by the other vector's length, you get the same scalar.
- **Distributive over vector addition**: $\mathbf{a} \cdot (\mathbf{b} + \mathbf{c}) = \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} + \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{c}$. Algebraically immediate from the component definition; geometrically, projecting onto $\mathbf{a}$'s direction is a linear operation, and linearity respects vector addition.
- **Compatible with scalar multiplication**: $(k\mathbf{a}) \cdot \mathbf{b} = k (\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b})$. Stretching $\mathbf{a}$ by $k$ stretches its shadow by $k$.

These three together make the dot product a **bilinear form** — linear in each argument when the other is fixed. That's a key reason it composes well with matrix-vector multiplication (next lesson up).

<Misconception belief="If a · b = 0, then either a is zero, b is zero, or both.">

The dot product can be zero even when **both vectors are non-zero** — that's precisely the orthogonality case. $(1, 0) \cdot (0, 1) = 0$ even though both vectors have magnitude 1. The dot product measures *agreement*, not magnitude; two non-zero vectors can simply have no agreement (i.e., be perpendicular).

</Misconception>

## Magnitudes and angles from dot products

Two consequences of the dot product worth memorising:

**Vector magnitude:** $|\mathbf{a}|^2 = \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{a}$. A vector dotted with itself gives its squared magnitude. Geometrically, the shadow of $\mathbf{a}$ on itself is exactly $\mathbf{a}$, and the formula collapses to $|\mathbf{a}| \cdot |\mathbf{a}| \cos 0 = |\mathbf{a}|^2$.

**Angle between two vectors:** $\cos\theta = \dfrac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{|\mathbf{a}| \, |\mathbf{b}|}$. Rearrange the geometric definition. This is *how* you compute the angle between two arbitrary vectors in any dimension — there's no other elementary way.

The angle formula generalises directly to $\mathbb{R}^n$: take the dot product (sum of $n$ component products), divide by the product of magnitudes (each computed as $\sqrt{\mathbf{x} \cdot \mathbf{x}}$). High-dimensional vectors don't *visually* have an angle, but they have a *cosine* — and the cosine is what most "similarity" measures in machine learning actually use. Two CLIP embeddings have an angle in 512-dimensional space; the cosine of that angle is what A-FINE's fidelity head consumes.

## Projection — the operation, not just the geometry

The shadow segment isn't just a visualisation; it's a *vector operation*. The **projection of $\mathbf{a}$ onto $\mathbf{b}$** is the vector pointing along $\mathbf{b}$'s direction with magnitude equal to the signed shadow length:

$$
\text{proj}_{\mathbf{b}} \mathbf{a} = \frac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{|\mathbf{b}|^2} \, \mathbf{b}
$$

The scalar coefficient $\dfrac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{|\mathbf{b}|^2}$ is *exactly* what the widget displays as the "projection scalar." Multiplying by $\mathbf{b}$ converts that scalar to a vector along $\mathbf{b}$'s line.

Projection is foundational: it underlies least-squares regression (projecting the data onto the column space of the design matrix), Gram-Schmidt orthogonalisation (subtracting projections to make orthogonal basis vectors), and the SVD (which decomposes a matrix into stretches along orthogonal projection directions).
