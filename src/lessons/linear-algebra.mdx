# Linear Algebra — Foundations

<LessonMeta tags={["MATHEMATICS", "LINEAR ALGEBRA", "FOUNDATIONS", "15–35 MIN"]} />

Pick up your phone, take a photograph, and watch what the camera does. It rotates the image to match the orientation you're holding the phone in. It corrects the lens distortion at the edges. It blends the exposure across frames. Every one of those steps is a *transformation*, and every transformation is doing the same kind of thing: it takes a collection of numbers (the pixels, or some representation of them), shuffles them around in a particular way, and produces a new collection of numbers. The language for talking about *those collections* and *those shufflings* is linear algebra. It runs every piece of modern computational mathematics — machine learning, computer graphics, physics simulation, signal processing — at the layer underneath the application.

Linear algebra is built on three primitive objects: scalars, vectors, and matrices. This lesson is the foundation: scalars and vectors. The matrices lesson follows separately, because matrices are different kinds of things — they're *transformations*, not just objects, and that shift in role deserves its own page.

<Tier level="standard">

The goal of this lesson isn't to memorise definitions. It's to develop the **geometric intuition** that lets you predict what a vector is going to do *before* you compute it. That intuition only comes from manipulating the objects yourself, not from reading about them. Every section below has a widget. Touch the widgets — the math will land harder than the prose can deliver on its own.

</Tier>

## Scalars — a single number, with units

A **scalar** is a single real number. Temperature, mass, speed, voltage — anything whose entire state is captured by *one* number. Mathematically we'll write scalars as lowercase letters in italic: $a, b, c, \alpha, \lambda$. They're the simplest objects in linear algebra, and they earn their name not from being numbers, but from what they *do*.

The two operations on scalars are the ones you've been using your whole life:

- **Addition**: $a + b$. Closed (the sum of two scalars is a scalar), commutative, associative.
- **Multiplication**: $a \cdot b$. Closed, commutative, associative, distributive over addition.

What makes a scalar a *scalar*, rather than just "a number", is the next layer of objects up. A scalar **scales** something — it multiplies a vector or a matrix, and in doing so it stretches them, shrinks them, or flips them. The scalar's whole job in linear algebra is to act on the other objects. That's why we give it a separate name.

<Tier level="standard">

> **Why insist on scalars versus vectors?** Because the operations are different, and confusing them is the most common first mistake in linear algebra. *Adding* two velocities is **not** scalar addition — velocity has a direction, and you have to add the directions too. Speed (the magnitude of velocity) *is* a scalar, but a 30 km/h east wind plus a 30 km/h west wind doesn't give 60 km/h; it gives zero net velocity. The directions cancelled, and the moment you collapsed velocity to its scalar magnitude (speed), you lost the information needed to see the cancellation. The next section is built to make sure you don't lose it.

## Vectors — a list of components, geometrically an arrow

A **vector** in $\mathbb{R}^2$ is an ordered pair of scalars: $\mathbf{v} = (v_1, v_2)$. In $\mathbb{R}^n$ it's an ordered $n$-tuple. We'll spend this lesson in $\mathbb{R}^2$ because it's the smallest dimension where the geometric intuition fully applies — but everything we say here generalises directly to higher dimensions. A 512-dimensional vector behaves the same way; you just can't draw it.

Two equivalent ways to *read* a vector, and you'll need both:

1. **Components**: $\mathbf{v} = (3, 2)$ means "three units along the first axis, two along the second". This is what you compute with.
2. **Geometric**: an arrow from the origin to the point $(3, 2)$. The arrow has a **magnitude** (its length, $|\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{v_1^2 + v_2^2}$) and a **direction** (the angle it makes with the positive $x$-axis, $\theta = \arctan(v_2 / v_1)$). This is what you reason geometrically about.

These aren't two different objects — they're two ways of looking at the same one. Pick up a vector in the widget below and drag its tip. Watch the components, magnitude, and angle all update together. They're describing the same arrow from different angles, and one of the things you want to build in your head over this lesson is the reflex to flip between them effortlessly.

### Magnitude ranker — your eye lies about length

Here's a small test. Six arrows are about to appear on a canvas. They have different lengths and they point in wildly different directions. You will be asked to drag them, top-to-bottom, in order of decreasing magnitude. Take a moment first to look at the canvas — try to do the ranking *visually*, the way you'd order objects by length on a desk.

Now look at the rows. The components $(v_x, v_y)$ are written next to each vector. The magnitude of a 2D vector is $|\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{v_x^2 + v_y^2}$ — Pythagoras, applied to the components. Two vectors with the same horizontal extent can have very different magnitudes if one is more sloped than the other. The (3, 4) trap is the canonical example: components that don't look impressive individually combine to a magnitude of exactly 5, longer than (4, 0) by more than 20%. Your eye is poorly calibrated for this. Diagonal length is not the sum of horizontal and vertical extents — it's the square root of their squared sum, which the visual system handles much worse than it handles axis-aligned lengths.

The traps the widget surfaces, in roughly the order they bite: ranking by horizontal extent ("the rightmost-pointing vector must be the longest"); ranking by visual altitude ("the highest tip means the largest magnitude"); ignoring negative components ("vectors that point left look short"). The submit reveals the true magnitudes; wrong rows are marked red with the actual $|\mathbf{v}|$ shown so you can see where your eye fooled you.

### Component reverse-engineer — read the arrow back into numbers

The usual exercise runs in one direction: you're given $(v_x, v_y)$ and you draw the arrow. The next widget runs in the *other* direction, which turns out to be the directional skill the rest of linear algebra rests on. An arrow is drawn from the origin to some hidden point. Your job is to type the components that produced it. The widget overlays your current guess as a ghost arrow on the same canvas — every time you nudge $v_x$ or $v_y$, the ghost moves, and you can see whether you're converging on the target or wandering off.

The mathematics is small and the practice is enormous. The arrow's tip is at the point $(v_x, v_y)$; $v_x$ is its horizontal distance from the origin, $v_y$ its vertical. That is the definition of components in $\mathbb{R}^2$ — but reading them off a drawn arrow is what you'll do over and over once matrices arrive (every column of a $2 \times 2$ matrix is exactly this kind of component pair, read off the matrix's image of a basis vector). Three tiers: integer components on easy, half-integers on medium, tenths on hard. When your guess matches within tolerance, the canvas flashes green and a new round generates automatically.

The traps it surfaces: confusing the sign of $v_y$ when the arrow points down (negative-$y$ vectors); estimating $v_x$ from the *projection onto the diagonal* rather than the actual horizontal extent (a slanted arrow's $v_x$ is its horizontal shadow, not its length); and on the hard tier, settling for a near-miss because the difference between $v_x = 1.2$ and $v_x = 1.3$ is genuinely hard to see by eye. The give-up button reveals the answer with dashed decomposition lines, so you can see exactly where your reading missed.

### Vector addition — head to tail

Vectors add **component-wise**: $\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b} = (a_1 + b_1, a_2 + b_2)$. That's the algebraic definition, and it's straightforward — add the x-components, add the y-components, you're done.

The geometric definition is more illuminating, and worth carrying around in your head as the *primary* mental model. To add $\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}$, place $\mathbf{b}$'s tail at $\mathbf{a}$'s tip. The sum is the arrow from $\mathbf{a}$'s tail to $\mathbf{b}$'s new tip. This is the **head-to-tail rule**, and the two definitions are guaranteed to produce the same answer — the algebraic version is just what the geometric version does to the coordinates.

The widget below makes this concrete. Two vectors $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$ are chained head-to-tail; the green resultant from the origin to the end of the chain is $\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}$. Drag either vector and watch the sum react.

Only if they point in **exactly the same direction**. The lesson-opening question made this point. In general, $|\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}|$ depends on the angle between $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$: equal-length opposing vectors sum to zero; perpendicular vectors give a Pythagorean sum; same-direction vectors give an additive sum. The **triangle inequality** $|\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}| \leq |\mathbf{a}| + |\mathbf{b}|$ holds always, with equality only in the same-direction case.

</Misconception>

### Chain construction — a puzzle for the head-to-tail rule

Now that you've felt the head-to-tail rule with two vectors, try it as a *puzzle*. The widget below gives you a target $\mathbf{T}$ and a palette of candidate vectors. Click palette tiles to chain them — each new vector starts where the previous one ended. The chain succeeds when the running sum lands on $\mathbf{T}$. Some puzzles can be solved with fewer segments than others; the widget tracks how close to optimal your chain is.

This is where commutativity and path-independence become visible. Two readers solving the same puzzle in different orders trace out *different paths through the plane* — but their final endpoints coincide, because vector addition doesn't care about the order. The path is the journey; the destination is the sum.

### Walls, budgets, and order — vector addition as embodied translation

Suppose you are standing on a tile floor and somebody hands you a small set of cards. Each card has an arrow on it: *step right*, *step up*, *step diagonally up-right*, *step left*. You have to reach a marked tile across the room. Some of the tiles between you and the goal are blocked off by furniture you cannot step through. You have eight moves before the lesson ends. Where do you go first?

This is the maze widget. The mathematics is exactly the same as the chain puzzle above — your position after $n$ moves is the sum of the $n$ chosen vectors — but the *embodiment* is different. You are no longer choosing arrows on a canvas to make a running sum match a target; you are physically navigating a grid one move at a time. The destination still doesn't care about order — three rights and two ups land you at $(3, 2)$ no matter which order you choose them in — but the *path* through walls does. Try reaching the goal in puzzle 3 with the wall in the way: the destination is still $(4, 2) = 4\hat{\imath} + 2\hat{\jmath}$, but you cannot get there by going right-first. The wall forces you to commit to an ordering, even though commutativity guarantees the sum doesn't notice.

The traps this surfaces: spending moves on detours without realising the budget will run out (greedy local choices that don't backtrack); forgetting that $-\hat{\imath}$ is an available move when the start position has already overshot the goal (puzzle 4 forces this — the goal is to the *left* of the start); and reflexively treating the action set as fixed rather than reading the palette each puzzle (puzzle 2 unlocks the diagonal $\hat{\imath}+\hat{\jmath}$, which collapses two moves into one).

### Scalar multiplication

Multiplying a vector by a scalar $k$ scales each component: $k\mathbf{v} = (k v_1, k v_2)$. That's the algebra. But the *meaning* — and the reason the operation is called "multiplication" in the first place — is **iterated addition**. When $k$ is a positive integer, $k\mathbf{v}$ is literally $\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{v} + \ldots + \mathbf{v}$, $k$ copies in a chain. The general definition for non-integer or negative $k$ just extends the integer rule (with a flip for $k < 0$), but the integer case is the conceptual anchor — if scalar multiplication ever feels mysterious, come back to "it's just adding $\mathbf{v}$ to itself a bunch of times".

The widget below makes that concrete. Two view modes:

- **Stacked copies** (default): draws $|k|$ copies of $\mathbf{v}$ head-to-tail (or copies of $-\mathbf{v}$ when $k$ is negative). $k = 3$ is literally three arrows in a chain; $k = 0.5$ is half an arrow. This is the iterated-addition picture.
- **Single stretched arrow**: draws $k\mathbf{v}$ as one arrow from the origin, stretched or flipped relative to $\mathbf{v}$. The standard textbook picture, cleaner but loses the iterated-addition meaning.

<ScalarMultiplier />

The patterns to feel by dragging:

- $k > 1$ — chain gets longer, direction unchanged.
- $0 < k < 1$ — chain gets shorter than $\mathbf{v}$ itself, still pointing the same way.
- $k = 0$ — chain collapses to a point at the origin. This single point is the **zero vector**, the additive identity, and it's worth remembering that every vector has it as a possible scaling.
- $k < 0$ — each copy reverses direction; the chain points the *opposite* way from $\mathbf{v}$. A "flipped" badge appears in the widget to flag it.
- $|k| = 1$ — length is preserved; $k = -1$ is a pure direction-flip with no stretching.

The magnitude rescales exactly: $|k\mathbf{v}| = |k| \cdot |\mathbf{v}|$. Notice the **absolute value** on $k$ — that detail is doing real work. Negative $k$ flips the vector's direction but does not make its magnitude negative.

<Misconception belief="If k is negative, then k·v has negative magnitude.">

Magnitudes are always non-negative — they measure *length*, which is a positive distance. $|k\mathbf{v}| = |k| \cdot |\mathbf{v}|$, with the absolute value applied to $k$. What negative $k$ flips is *direction*, not magnitude. A vector pointing 30° east of north, scaled by $-1$, points 30° west of south — same length, opposite heading.

</Misconception>

### Speed-match — three seconds, pick the scalar

You have built the intuition slowly. Now compress it. The widget below flashes a vector $\mathbf{v}$ and a target $k\mathbf{v}$ on the canvas, drops four scalar candidates underneath, and gives you three seconds to choose. The mechanics of the operation haven't changed — $k$ scales the length, the sign decides the direction — but the *time budget* has. Three seconds is not enough to compute the ratio carefully. It is just enough to *see* it.

This is the point of the round. The scalar you reach for under pressure is the one your intuition has already wired in. If you find yourself reaching for the wrong sign when $k$ is negative, or guessing $k = 2$ when the target is barely longer than $\mathbf{v}$, that's the feedback the widget exists to surface — your eye hasn't calibrated to length-and-direction yet, and the timer is the thing forcing the calibration. Three difficulty tiers: integer scalars on easy, half-integers on medium, decimals with 0.3-step distractors on hard.

The traps the widget surfaces, in roughly the order they bite: missing the sign on negative $k$ (the target points the opposite way, your eye still picks a positive scalar of the same magnitude); confusing $k$ with $|k|$ when the target is shorter (picking $0.5$ when the answer is $-0.5$); over-reading the visual length under time pressure and picking $k = 3$ when the answer is $k = 2$. None of these are computation errors — they are *intuition* errors, which is exactly what three-second rounds expose.

### The dot product — a way to multiply two vectors into a scalar

There's no straightforward way to "multiply" two vectors in $\mathbb{R}^2$ and get another vector — the candidate definitions all run into problems. What you *can* do is define operations that take two vectors and produce *something else*, and the most fundamental of those operations is the **dot product**, which produces a single scalar:

$$
\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = a_1 b_1 + a_2 b_2
$$

This algebraic form has a geometric equivalent that's a much better mental anchor:

$$
\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = |\mathbf{a}| \, |\mathbf{b}| \cos\theta
$$

where $\theta$ is the angle between the two vectors. That these two expressions describe the same number is the dot product's central result — and the second expression reads naturally:

- If the vectors are **parallel** ($\theta = 0$), $\cos\theta = 1$, so the dot product is the product of the magnitudes.
- If the vectors are **perpendicular** ($\theta = 90°$), $\cos\theta = 0$, so the dot product is zero.
- If the vectors are **anti-parallel** ($\theta = 180°$), $\cos\theta = -1$, so the dot product is the *negative* of the magnitude product.

The dot product is **the** test for orthogonality. Two non-zero vectors are perpendicular if and only if their dot product is zero. The dedicated [Linear Algebra — The Dot Product](#/lesson/linear-algebra-dot-product) lesson develops the projection geometry, the relationship between the dot product and the angle, and the role the dot product plays in matrix-vector multiplication.
