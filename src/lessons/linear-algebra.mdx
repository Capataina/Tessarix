# Linear Algebra Primer — Scalars, Vectors, Matrices

<LessonMeta tags={["MATHEMATICS", "LINEAR ALGEBRA", "FOUNDATIONS", "20–60 MIN"]} />

Linear algebra is the language for talking about quantities that have **multiple components** and the **transformations** that act on them. Most of modern computational mathematics — machine learning, computer graphics, physics simulation, signal processing — runs on three primitive objects: scalars, vectors, and matrices. This lesson builds them up in order, with each one earning its keep by *doing* something the previous one couldn't.

<Tier level="standard">

The goal isn't to memorise definitions. It's to develop the **geometric intuition** that lets you predict what a vector or matrix will do before you compute it — and that intuition only forms by *manipulating* the objects, not by reading about them. Every section below has a widget; touch the widgets.

</Tier>

## Scalars — a single number, with units

A **scalar** is a single real number. Temperature, mass, speed, voltage — anything whose state is fully captured by *one* number. Mathematically we'll just write scalars as lowercase letters in normal italic: $a, b, c, \alpha, \lambda$.

The two operations that matter are familiar:

- **Addition**: $a + b$. Closed (sum of two scalars is a scalar), commutative, associative.
- **Multiplication**: $a \cdot b$. Closed, commutative, associative, distributive over addition.

What makes a scalar a *scalar*, rather than just "a number," is that we use it to **scale** something else — multiply a vector by it, multiply a matrix by it. The scalar's job in linear algebra is to act on other objects.

<Tier level="standard">

> **Why insist on scalars vs vectors?** Because the operations differ. *Adding* two velocities is **not** scalar addition: velocity has a direction. Speed (the magnitude of velocity) is a scalar — but a 30 km/h east wind plus a 30 km/h west wind doesn't give 60 km/h; it gives zero net velocity. The "direction" has been lost when you collapse to a scalar. This is the slip the next section is built to fix.

</Tier>

## Vectors — a list of components, geometrically an arrow

A **vector** in $\mathbb{R}^2$ is an ordered pair of scalars: $\mathbf{v} = (v_1, v_2)$. In $\mathbb{R}^n$ it's an ordered $n$-tuple. We'll work in $\mathbb{R}^2$ throughout this lesson because it's the smallest dimension where geometric intuition fully applies — but everything generalises.

Two equivalent ways to *read* a vector:

1. **Components**: $\mathbf{v} = (3, 2)$ means "three units along the first axis, two along the second." This is what you compute with.
2. **Geometric**: an arrow from the origin to the point $(3, 2)$. Has a **magnitude** (its length, $|\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{v_1^2 + v_2^2}$) and a **direction** (the angle it makes with the positive $x$-axis, $\theta = \arctan(v_2 / v_1)$). This is what you reason geometrically about.

### Vector addition — head to tail

Vectors add **component-wise**: $\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b} = (a_1 + b_1, a_2 + b_2)$. That's the algebraic definition. The geometric definition is more illuminating: to add $\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}$, place $\mathbf{b}$'s tail at $\mathbf{a}$'s tip. The sum is the arrow from $\mathbf{a}$'s tail to $\mathbf{b}$'s new tip. This is the **head-to-tail** rule, and the two definitions are guaranteed to give the same answer.

The widget below shows this directly. Two vectors $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$ chain head-to-tail; the green resultant from the origin to the chain's end is $\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}$.

<Misconception belief="The sum of two vectors of length 3 and length 5 always has length 8.">

Only if they point in **exactly the same direction**. The lesson-opening question made this point. In general, $|\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}|$ depends on the angle between $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$: equal-length opposing vectors sum to zero; perpendicular vectors give a Pythagorean sum; same-direction vectors give an additive sum. The **triangle inequality** $|\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}| \leq |\mathbf{a}| + |\mathbf{b}|$ holds always, with equality only in the same-direction case.

</Misconception>

### Scalar multiplication

Multiplying a vector by a scalar $k$ scales each component: $k \mathbf{v} = (k v_1, k v_2)$. That's the algebra. But the *meaning* — and the reason the operation is called "multiplication" in the first place — is iterated addition: $k \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{v} + \mathbf{v} + \ldots + \mathbf{v}$ ($k$ times) when $k$ is a positive integer. For non-integer or negative $k$ we extend the definition by the obvious rule (and a flip for $k < 0$), but the integer case is the conceptual anchor.

The widget below makes this concrete. Two view modes:

- **Stacked copies** (default): draws $|k|$ copies of $\mathbf{v}$ head-to-tail (or copies of $-\mathbf{v}$ when $k$ is negative). $k = 3$ is literally three arrows in a chain; $k = 0.5$ is half an arrow.
- **Single stretched arrow**: draws $k\mathbf{v}$ as one arrow from the origin, stretched or flipped relative to $\mathbf{v}$. The standard textbook picture.

<ScalarMultiplier />

The patterns to feel:

- $k > 1$ — chain gets longer, direction unchanged.
- $0 < k < 1$ — chain gets shorter than $\mathbf{v}$ itself, direction unchanged.
- $k = 0$ — chain collapses to a point at the origin (the **zero vector**).
- $k < 0$ — each copy reverses direction; the chain points the *opposite* way from $\mathbf{v}$. A "flipped" badge appears.
- $|k| = 1$ — length preserved; $k = -1$ specifically is a pure direction-flip.

The magnitude rescales exactly: $|k\mathbf{v}| = |k| \cdot |\mathbf{v}|$. Note the **absolute value** — negative $k$ flips direction but doesn't make the magnitude negative.

<Misconception belief="If k is negative, then k·v has negative magnitude.">

Magnitudes are always non-negative — they measure *length*, which is a positive distance. $|k\mathbf{v}| = |k| \cdot |\mathbf{v}|$, with the absolute value applied to $k$. What negative $k$ flips is *direction*, not magnitude. A vector pointing 30° east of north, scaled by $-1$, points 30° west of south — same length, opposite heading.

</Misconception>

### The dot product — a way to multiply two vectors into a scalar

There's no "vector multiplication" in $\mathbb{R}^2$ in the way scalars multiply. There are several *products of two vectors*, and the most fundamental is the **dot product**, which collapses two vectors to a scalar:

$$
\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = a_1 b_1 + a_2 b_2
$$

This algebraic form has a geometric equivalent:

$$
\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = |\mathbf{a}| \, |\mathbf{b}| \cos\theta
$$

where $\theta$ is the angle between the two vectors. Reading the geometric form:

- If the vectors are **parallel** ($\theta = 0$), $\cos\theta = 1$, so the dot product is the product of the magnitudes.
- If the vectors are **perpendicular** ($\theta = 90°$), $\cos\theta = 0$, so the dot product is zero.
- If the vectors are **anti-parallel** ($\theta = 180°$), $\cos\theta = -1$, so the dot product is the *negative* of the magnitude product.

The dot product is **the** test for orthogonality: $\mathbf{a} \perp \mathbf{b}$ if and only if $\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = 0$.

</Tier>

## Matrices — a transformation of vectors

A **matrix** is a rectangular grid of scalars. A $2 \times 2$ matrix has 4 entries:

$$
A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}
$$

Algebraically, a matrix and a vector multiply like this:

$$
A \mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} ax + by \\ cx + dy \end{pmatrix}
$$

That's the calculation. But the *meaning* is geometric: a $2 \times 2$ matrix is a **linear transformation of the plane**. Every point in $\mathbb{R}^2$ gets mapped to a new point. Lines stay lines (curves never appear). The origin stays at the origin (the zero vector is always fixed). Parallel lines stay parallel.

The cleanest way to *see* a matrix is to ask: **where does it send the basis vectors?** The standard basis is $\hat{\imath} = (1, 0)$ and $\hat{\jmath} = (0, 1)$. After the transformation:

- $\hat{\imath} \mapsto (a, c)$ — the matrix's first column
- $\hat{\jmath} \mapsto (b, d)$ — the matrix's second column

Every other point in the plane is some combination of $\hat{\imath}$ and $\hat{\jmath}$, so once you know where the basis vectors go, the rest of the plane follows by linearity. **A $2 \times 2$ matrix is fully specified by where it sends $\hat{\imath}$ and $\hat{\jmath}$.**

### Determinants — the area-scaling factor

The **determinant** of a $2 \times 2$ matrix tells you how the matrix scales **areas**:

$$
\det A = ad - bc
$$

Geometrically: the unit square has area 1; after the transformation it becomes a parallelogram whose (signed) area is exactly $\det A$. Three special cases worth feeling:

- $\det A = 1$: the transformation preserves area exactly. Rotations and reflections are like this.
- $\det A = 0$: the transformation **collapses** the plane onto a line (or a point). The unit square's image has zero area. The matrix is called **singular** in this case — it can't be inverted, because once you've collapsed the plane, no transformation can recover the original.
- $\det A < 0$: the transformation **flips orientation** — the unit square's image traversed in the original order goes around the parallelogram in the opposite direction. Reflections give this; pure rotations don't.

<Misconception belief="A matrix with determinant zero just has a really small effect.">

A determinant of zero means the matrix is **singular** — the transformation collapses the entire plane onto a lower-dimensional subspace (a line, or in the $\mathbf{0}$ matrix case, a single point). All of $\mathbb{R}^2$ now lives in a 1D image. This is a fundamentally different regime from "small effect" — it's an *irreversible* operation. You can't recover the original vector from its image because infinitely many original vectors map to the same image point. Algebraically: singular matrices have no inverse.

</Misconception>

### Composition of transformations is matrix multiplication

Applying transformation $A$ then transformation $B$ to a vector $\mathbf{v}$ gives $B(A\mathbf{v})$. The associativity of matrix multiplication means this equals $(BA)\mathbf{v}$ — there's a single matrix $BA$ that captures the combined transformation. **Matrix multiplication is exactly the algebraic encoding of composing geometric transformations.**

Order matters: $AB \ne BA$ in general. Rotating first and then shearing is different from shearing first and then rotating — try it on a unit square in your head.

## Where to read next

The objects in this lesson — scalars, vectors, matrices — are the **building blocks**. Real applications combine them in two layers:

- **Higher dimensions**: everything generalises to $\mathbb{R}^n$ for any $n$. The vectors get longer, the matrices get bigger, but the geometric intuition transfers: matrices still transform space, determinants still scale (signed) volumes.
- **Compositions and decompositions**: eigenvalues, singular value decomposition, change of basis. These are the next layer up — they explain *what kind of transformation* a given matrix represents, in a way that's invariant to coordinate choice.
